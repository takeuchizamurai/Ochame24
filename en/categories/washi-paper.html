Washi Paper

The Miracle of Light-Transmitting Thinness, Spun from Plant Fibre

A Memory Written in Fibre, Lasting a Thousand Years

When you hold a sheet of washi up to the light, something remarkable happens. The fibres — long, interlocking strands of kozo, mitsumata, or gampi — become visible as a living web, a map of the hands and water and patience that made it. This is paper as it has been made in Japan for over thirteen centuries, unchanged in its essential nature even as the world around it has been transformed beyond recognition.

Documents written on washi at Horyuji Temple in Nara have survived more than a thousand years and remain perfectly legible today. This is no accident. Unlike Western paper, which is made with acidic processes and deteriorates within centuries, washi is produced in an alkaline environment that brings the fibres close to neutral pH. The result is a material of extraordinary longevity — quiet, modest, and built to outlast the civilisations that created it.

Three Plants, Three Personalities

The character of washi begins in the field, with the choice of plant. Kozo (paper mulberry) produces long, robust fibres that yield a strong, textured sheet — ideal for calligraphy, woodblock printing, and the translucent screens of shoji doors. Mitsumata (Oriental paperbush) gives a finer, silkier result with a gentle sheen; its quality was so prized that it was used for many decades as the raw material for Japanese banknotes.

Gampi, the rarest of the three, is difficult to cultivate and grows only in the wild. It produces a sheet of exceptional thinness and translucency with a natural resistance to insects — a property that made it the material of choice for preserving precious documents over the centuries. Each plant asks something different of the papermaker, and each rewards the skilled hand with a paper unlike any other.

The making process itself is a study in disciplined craft. The plant bark is steamed, beaten, and released into water mixed with a natural mucilage derived from the tororo-aoi plant. The papermaker then rocks a bamboo screen — the sugeta — through the milky liquid in a flowing, rhythmic motion known as nagashi-zuki. This uniquely Japanese technique allows fibres to cross in multiple directions, giving the finished sheet its characteristic combination of strength and suppleness.

The Three Great Producing Regions

Echizen Washi
Fukui Prefecture

One of Japan's oldest papermaking centres, with a history spanning more than 1,500 years. Echizen has long produced ceremonial papers for the imperial court, and is home to Okamoto Shrine — the only shrine in Japan dedicated to the deity of papermaking.

Mino Washi
Gifu Prefecture

The "Mino-ban" sheet size standardised here during the Edo period became a national benchmark. Known for its thin, even surface, Mino washi is deeply woven into daily life through shoji screens and lanterns, and continues to be prized by artists worldwide.

Tosa Washi
Kochi Prefecture

Drawing on the pristine waters of the Niyodo River in Shikoku, Tosa washi is celebrated for its durability. Conservation specialists at institutions including the Louvre and the Smithsonian rely on Tosa sheets to restore fragile works of art and historic documents.

A UNESCO Heritage, and What That Means

In November 2014, UNESCO inscribed "Washi, craftsmanship of traditional Japanese hand papermaking" on its Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. The recognised traditions were those of Sekishu-Banshi from Shimane, Hon-Minoshi from Gifu, and Hosokawashi from Saitama — three communities where the knowledge and technique have been passed down through generations without interruption.

The inscription was significant not merely as an honour, but as a recognition of something deeper: that washi is not simply a product, but a living relationship between people, plants, water, and time. The evaluation noted the craft's low reliance on chemical inputs, its integration with local ecosystems, and the way knowledge is transmitted organically within communities. In an era when sustainability has become an urgent concern, washi had been practicing it quietly for centuries.

Washi in the Contemporary World

Washi has never been content to remain in the past. Today it is experiencing a remarkable renaissance, crossing into fields as diverse as fashion, architecture, fine art, and technology. Japanese and international designers have incorporated washi textiles into clothing collections, drawn by the material's warmth, breathability, and the subtle variation of its surface. Architects deploy it in interior panels and lighting fixtures, exploiting its ability to filter and soften light in ways that no synthetic material can replicate.

Photographers and printmakers increasingly choose washi as their preferred substrate, finding that its texture and absorbency lend images a depth and warmth absent from conventional papers. Meanwhile, the global art restoration community has come to depend on it: delicate washi sheets are used to back and reinforce everything from medieval manuscripts to oil paintings, their neutrality and strength making them uniquely suited to the task.

"Paper is the mirror of civilisation. Within the fibres of washi lies the history of a people who learned to listen to nature — and, through patience, to draw beauty from it."
