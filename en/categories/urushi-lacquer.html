Japanese Urushi Lacquerware

A Thousand Years of Light and Craft

A Story That Begins in the Jomon Period

At an archaeological site called Kakinoshima in Hokkaido, Japan, researchers uncovered a lacquered burial offering dating back approximately 9,000 years. It is the oldest known example of urushi lacquerware in Japan — proof that even the people of the Jomon period understood the remarkable properties of this material and had mastered its use.

Since then, lacquerware has accompanied every era of Japanese life: furnishing the halls of temples and shrines, adorning samurai armor, and gracing the everyday tables of common households. Its unbroken continuity through millennia is no accident — it is a testament to the singular qualities of urushi itself.

A Natural Miracle — What Is Urushi?

Urushi is the sap collected from the urushi tree (Toxicodendron vernicifluum) by carefully wounding its bark. A single tree yields only about 200 grams of sap per year. A traditional saying captures the effort: "a hundred cuts for one gō, a thousand cuts for one shō" — meaning it takes an enormous number of incisions to collect even small amounts. This scarcity makes urushi one of Japan's most precious natural materials.

When this sap is exposed to air and humidity, it undergoes a remarkable chemical process — enzymatic oxidation that transforms it into an incredibly durable polymer. Fully cured urushi is harder than plastic, resistant to water, acids, and heat, and can maintain its luminous finish for hundreds of years. Ancient urushi artifacts excavated from sites across Japan remain perfectly intact to this day.

Depth Through Layers

Undercoat, middle coat, top coat — a fine lacquer piece can involve over 100 individual layers of urushi, each carefully applied and then polished smooth before the next is added. This painstaking process of layering and refining is the source of lacquerware's signature luminous depth and of the quality that allows it to grow more beautiful with age and use.

Regional Styles — Each with Its Own Character

Across Japan, distinct regional schools have evolved their own techniques and aesthetics, each shaped by local materials, history, and culture.

Wajima Nuri (Ishikawa)

One of Japan's three great lacquer traditions, renowned for its extraordinary durability. A single bowl passes through 124 distinct processes. Famous for makie (gold powder painting) and chinkin (engraved gold inlay) decoration.

Aizu Lacquer (Fukushima)

With 400 years of history, Aizu lacquer was promoted as an industry by the feudal lord Gamo Ujisato. Known for its vivid vermilion and black contrasts and a wide range of accessible designs suited to everyday use.

Echizen Nuri (Fukui)

Boasting 1,500 years of history, Echizen lacquerware originated as offerings to Shinto shrines. It is prized for its robust construction and a clean, understated elegance that has endured through the centuries.

Kiso Lacquerware (Nagano)

Flourishing along the historic Nakasendo road in the Edo period, Kiso ware uses lightweight local cypress as its base. Its signature rust-clay undercoat gives it a unique character perfectly suited to everyday dining.

A Vessel That Grows with You

One of urushi lacquerware's most remarkable qualities is that it improves with use. Every time you pick up a lacquer bowl, wash it gently, and dry it with care, the coating stabilizes a little more. The luster deepens. It is as though the vessel is slowly becoming yours — a quality unlike any other material.

Today, a new generation of Japanese artists is pushing urushi into unexpected territory: using it as a painting medium, combining it with metal and resin, and producing bold modern forms that carry the ancient material into contemporary life. Old and new, traditional and experimental — urushi adapts without losing its essence.

Choosing and Living with Urushi

When selecting authentic lacquerware, look for pieces labeled "天然漆使用" (made with natural urushi). Care is simple: avoid the microwave and dishwasher, wash gently with a soft sponge and mild detergent, and wipe dry immediately. With this minimal attention, a quality piece of urushi lacquerware will last for decades — and if it is ever scratched or worn, a skilled lacquer craftsman can restore it to its original condition.

To hold a lacquer bowl is to hold two timescales at once: 9,000 years of a craft that has survived every upheaval of history, and the several months of concentrated effort a single artisan poured into its creation. Few objects carry that weight so lightly.
